Thoughts on Topics in the Digital Humanities Course

An essay on TEI, Open Access, and GLAM Digitisation

This essay will discuss topics in the course, reflecting on TEI-XML and other text encoding scenarios, the problems and controversy of open access, and digitisation in the GLAM sector.

1. TEI and XML

HTML and XML are both mark-up languages, and there are other mark-up languages such as YAML. Different from HTML, which is based on XML, XML is only targeted at storing text information, preserving the hierarchical traits of the text, but not containing anything about displaying the text.

XML is used in many places in today’s digital lives of people. For instance, files like .docx, .xlsx, .pptx are essentially ZIP archives containing many XML files. If you rename a .docx file to .zip and extract it, you will find a collection of XML files.

Beyond TEI-XML, there are other encoding frameworks:

  • EpiDoc – an XML standard built on TEI, used for inscriptions, papyri, and ancient documents.
  • MEI (Music Encoding Initiative) – a TEI-like encoding for music notation.
  • IIIF (International Image Interoperability Framework) – not a mark-up language, but a standard enabling rich digital presentation of manuscripts and images across institutions.
  • YAML / JSON for born-digital archives – used increasingly in digital asset management systems because they are lightweight and machine-friendly. Reimann et al. (2024) notably argue that capturing born-digital data, such as online forum threads with emojis and upvotes, requires custom schemas that reflect the nested, tree-like structure of digital conversations rather than traditional linear text.
  • Text mining corpora – often encoded in stand-off annotation formats rather than TEI to allow multiple layers of annotation.
TEI preserves scholarly nuance, but it is labour-intensive, which means large-scale TEI projects are more likely to be accomplished by well-resourced institutions.

Furthermore, the standards within TEI are not static. While the flexibility of TEI is a strength, maintaining the ODD (One Document Does it All) processor—which generates schemas and documentation—has proven difficult, leading to recent initiatives like "ATOP" to rebuild ODD processing from scratch (Bauman et al., 2024). Additionally, TEI continues to evolve socially; Beshero-Bondar et al. (2024) highlight recent updates to the guidelines that move away from binary, ISO-standard definitions of sex and gender, allowing for more culturally accurate and inclusive personography in digital editions.

2. Open Access – The Internet’s Own Boy

I learnt a lot from the problem discussed in this film, which I think is about the accessibility of research journal articles, which is also related to copyright issues. After watching the film and engaging in our class, my understanding now is that the copyright of journal articles usually belongs to the publishers (e.g. Elsevier, JSTOR). Am I right? Then the question is whether it is fair for the publishers to charge.

The current inequality in access to knowledge for the public and scholars in educational institutions is in part because publishers charge for access. Scholars in institutions have privileged access because their institutions subscribe. I agree that we should find a better model to give more equal access to knowledge to everyone. I also know that some scholars use funding to pay APCs so their publications can be open access.

However, the "Gold Open Access" model, which relies on Article Processing Charges (APCs), introduces new inequities. Papillon et al. (2019) argue that while this solves the readability issue, it essentially transfers the cost barrier from the reader to the author. This disenfranchises researchers in the humanities and social sciences who often lack large grants, as well as junior scholars and those in the Global South. A purely APC-driven model risks creating a "pay-to-publish" system where only well-funded voices are heard.

Beyond journal articles, I am also concerned about the status of books in the open-access debate. While journal articles typically generate no income for their authors—who are usually salaried researchers—books belong to a different economic and labour structure. Monographs often represent years of work, and authors may rely, even modestly, on royalties as part of their income. This makes the ethics of providing free access to books more complicated than those surrounding journal articles. At the same time, students and independent learners frequently struggle with the high cost of academic books, which partly explains the appeal of websites like Z-Library, even though many of these platforms operate illegally.

Furthermore, Open Access in the GLAM sector faces unique challenges regarding what constitutes a "research output." Rees Koerner and Ackrell (2025) note that while universities produce papers, museums produce exhibitions, labels, and engagement activities. Traditional repositories are ill-equipped to capture these ephemeral, multi-modal outputs, requiring new infrastructure to make museum research truly open and transparent.

My own experience reflects this tension: as a consumer of intellectual resources, I appreciate the accessibility that piracy provides, yet I remain cautious about participating in it because of the legal risks and the potential impact on authors whose livelihood may depend on book sales. These differences suggest that open access cannot adopt a single, uniform model, and that books require a more nuanced and sustainable approach.

3. Digitisation in the GLAM sector

The digitisation in GLAMs provides many benefits. For example, it helps with scholarly studies. In the past year, I have been obsessed with two research works by a group of visualisation (as a part of computer science) researchers. Their works OldVisOnline and ZuantuSet retrieve images about historical visualisations from major digital collections around the world and build them as a database.

Zhang et al. (2023) note in their creation of OldVisOnline that historical visualizations are often scattered across disparate institutions (libraries, museums, archives) with inconsistent metadata, making a unified dataset crucial for studying the visual history of science. Furthermore, Mei et al. (2025) highlight a critical gap in digital humanities: the underrepresentation of non-Western materials. Their ZuantuSet specifically addresses Chinese historical visualizations, challenging the Eurocentric bias often found in generalist datasets and requiring specific taxonomy adjustments to capture unique artistic styles.

However, despite thirty years of investment, only about 22% of heritage collections in Europe are digitized (Terras, 2022). Crucially, even when objects are digitized, they are often difficult to find or use due to poor metadata practices. Lim and Liew (2011) found that while many NZ institutions use Simple Dublin Core, the lack of technical metadata and the reliance on proprietary systems creates silos where data cannot be easily shared or harvested. Without interoperable metadata, digitized items effectively remain invisible.

Digitisation itself encompasses a wide range of technical methods. Traditional techniques include 2D scanning of flat objects (manuscripts, prints), high‑resolution photography for artworks, and multispectral imaging used to reveal erased or faded text. More advanced projects use photogrammetry, which reconstructs 3D geometry from many photographs, and structured‑light or laser scanning, which produce highly accurate 3D meshes of objects such as sculptures, scientific instruments, or archaeological artefacts.

While these technologies are impressive, they are not without limitations. Tausch et al. (2019) describe the immense challenges of automating 3D digitization for mass collections, particularly for objects with reflective surfaces like gold, which require specialized polarization techniques. Furthermore, the accessibility of the resulting images is often overstated. Valeonti et al. (2019) note that many "Open Access" museums still provide only low-resolution images or wrap their content in cumbersome download forms to track usage, creating friction that discourages reuse.

Digitisation is often legally blocked because scanning or copying an artwork, book, or photograph constitutes making a “copy” under copyright law. GLAM institutions often cannot digitise works unless they can find (or pay) the copyright holders. Because of these constraints, institutions sometimes digitise items based on the ease of rights clearance rather than cultural significance. Coad (2019) describes this phenomenon as the "digital skew," where our online cultural memory is dominated by older, public domain works, while modern history remains hidden behind copyright walls. This creates a distorted view of history where the "digital public domain" does not accurately reflect our recent past.

Digitisation also enables digital repatriation, which can be a powerful tool for indigenous communities seeking access to cultural artefacts that cannot be physically returned.

However, we must remain critical of the process, ensuring that digitization efforts prioritize data quality, interoperability, and legal frameworks that allow for true accessibility, rather than creating new digital silos.
