Reflections on Topics in the DH6033 Course

An essay on TEI, Open Access, and GLAM Digitisation

Guang Yang

This essay reflects on key topics covered in the course DH6033. While the course covered a broad range of concepts, this discussion will focus on three specific areas: TEI-XML and text encoding frameworks, the complexities and controversies surrounding Open Access, and digitisation practices within the GLAM (Galleries, Libraries, Archives, and Museums) sector. These topics were selected due to their specific relevance to my interests and the significant scholarly debates they continue to generate within the field.

1. TEI and XML

Markup languages form the backbone of digital text processing. While HTML (HyperText Markup Language) focuses on the presentation and display of data, XML (eXtensible Markup Language) is designed specifically for the storage and transport of structured data, preserving the hierarchical semantic structure of the content rather than its appearance.

XML is ubiquitous in modern digital infrastructure, often operating invisibly to the end user. For instance, common file formats such as .docx, .xlsx, and .pptx are essentially ZIP archives containing collections of XML files that describe the document's content and structure. By renaming a .docx file to .zip and extracting it, one can view the underlying XML architecture.

In the Digital Humanities, the Text Encoding Initiative (TEI) is the de facto standard for XML implementation. However, the landscape of encoding frameworks is diverse, with specific standards developed to address unique material constraints:

  • MEI (Music Encoding Initiative): A community-driven effort to define a TEI-like encoding scheme for music notation.
  • IIIF (International Image Interoperability Framework): While not a markup language itself, this standard enables the rich digital presentation and interoperability of manuscripts and images across disparate institutions.
  • YAML / JSON for born-digital archives: Lightweight, machine-friendly formats increasingly used in digital asset management. Reimann et al. (2024) notably argue that capturing born-digital data, such as online forum threads replete with emojis and upvotes, often requires custom schemas that reflect the nested, tree-like structure of digital conversations, a complexity that traditional linear text encoding can struggle to capture effectively.
  • Text mining corpora: These are often encoded using stand-off annotation formats rather than embedded TEI to allow for multiple, overlapping layers of interpretation without breaking the XML hierarchy.
TEI preserves scholarly nuance, but it is labour-intensive, which means large-scale TEI projects are more likely to be accomplished by well-resourced institutions.

Furthermore, TEI is not a static standard; it evolves to meet both technical and social challenges. Bauman et al. (2024) highlight the technical difficulties in maintaining the ODD (One Document Does it All) processor, noting that the complexity of the existing TEI Stylesheets has necessitated new initiatives like "ATOP" to rebuild processing tools from scratch. Simultaneously, the guidelines evolve to reflect changing cultural values. Beshero-Bondar et al. (2024) detail recent revisions to the guidelines that move away from binary, ISO-standard definitions of sex and gender, empowering encoders to use more culturally accurate and inclusive personography in digital editions.

A compelling application of these standards is the Early Manila Hokkien project, which is developing a multi-layered digital edition of the 17th-century Chinese-Spanish dictionary Bocabulario de lengua sangleya. By leveraging TEI, the researchers encode distinct layers of analysis, from diplomatic transcriptions to historical tonal features and modern translations. This process allows for a rich reconstruction of the language used by Chinese immigrants in early Manila (Scholger et al., 2025). Below is an example of the TEI-XML encoding used in their research.

2. Open Access

The documentary The Internet’s Own Boy serves as a poignant introduction to the structural tensions inherent in academic knowledge production. It highlights the restrictive nature of copyright laws that govern scholarly journal articles and notes that these rights typically reside not with the creators of the knowledge but with commercial publishers such as Elsevier and JSTOR. This raises a fundamental ethical question: is it just for publishers to commodify knowledge that is largely produced through public funding and academic labour?

The current landscape creates a tiered system of information privilege. While those affiliated with well-funded institutions enjoy seamless access via library subscriptions, the general public and independent scholars remain effectively locked out. While the move toward Open Access seeks to remedy this, the transition is fraught with economic complexity.

Specifically, the "Gold Open Access" model, which often relies on Article Processing Charges (APCs), risks introducing new forms of inequity. Papillon et al. (2019) argue that while this model solves the issue of accessibility for the reader, it essentially transfers the financial barrier to the author. This shift threatens to disenfranchise researchers in the humanities and social sciences who often lack large grants, as well as junior scholars and those in the Global South, potentially creating a "pay-to-publish" system where only well-funded voices can afford to be open.

Furthermore, the debate requires nuance when distinguishing between different formats of scholarship. While journal articles typically generate no direct income for their authors, monographs operate within a different economic structure. Books often represent years of labour, and their authors may rely on royalties as a component of their livelihood. Consequently, the ethics of mandating free access to books are more complicated than those surrounding journal articles. Yet, the high cost of academic monographs continues to drive students and independent learners toward "shadow libraries" like Z-Library, despite the legal and ethical grey areas they occupy.

The definition of what constitutes a "research output" further complicates the application of Open Access principles, particularly within the GLAM sector. Rees Koerner and Ackrell (2025) note that while universities primarily produce textual publications, museums generate multi-modal outputs such as exhibitions, gallery labels, and engagement activities. Traditional digital repositories are often ill-equipped to capture these ephemeral outputs, necessitating new infrastructure to ensure that museum research is as transparent and accessible as its academic counterpart.

This distinction creates a personal tension: as a consumer of intellectual resources, I appreciate the immediate accessibility provided by various digital platforms, yet I remain cautious about participating in systems that might economically harm authors of monographs. These differences suggest that Open Access cannot adopt a monolithic model; rather, books and non-traditional outputs require distinct, sustainable approaches that balance accessibility with the economic realities of production.

3. Digitisation in the GLAM sector

The digitisation in GLAMs provides many benefits. For example, it helps with scholarly studies. In the past year, I have been obsessed with two research works by a group of visualisation (as a part of computer science) researchers. Their works OldVisOnline and ZuantuSet retrieve images about historical visualisations from major digital collections around the world and build them as a database.

Zhang et al. (2023) note in their creation of OldVisOnline that historical visualizations are often scattered across disparate institutions (libraries, museums, archives) with inconsistent metadata, making a unified dataset crucial for studying the visual history of science. Furthermore, Mei et al. (2025) highlight a critical gap in digital humanities: the underrepresentation of non-Western materials. Their ZuantuSet specifically addresses Chinese historical visualizations, challenging the Eurocentric bias often found in generalist datasets and requiring specific taxonomy adjustments to capture unique artistic styles.

However, despite thirty years of investment, only about 22% of heritage collections in Europe are digitized (Terras, 2022). Crucially, even when objects are digitized, they are often difficult to find or use due to poor metadata practices. Lim and Liew (2011) found that while many NZ institutions use Simple Dublin Core, the lack of technical metadata and the reliance on proprietary systems creates silos where data cannot be easily shared or harvested. Without interoperable metadata, digitized items effectively remain invisible.

Digitisation itself encompasses a wide range of technical methods. Traditional techniques include 2D scanning of flat objects (manuscripts, prints), high‑resolution photography for artworks, and multispectral imaging used to reveal erased or faded text. More advanced projects use photogrammetry, which reconstructs 3D geometry from many photographs, and structured‑light or laser scanning, which produce highly accurate 3D meshes of objects such as sculptures, scientific instruments, or archaeological artefacts.

While these technologies are impressive, they are not without limitations. Tausch et al. (2019) describe the immense challenges of automating 3D digitization for mass collections, particularly for objects with reflective surfaces like gold, which require specialized polarization techniques. Furthermore, the accessibility of the resulting images is often overstated. Valeonti et al. (2019) note that many "Open Access" museums still provide only low-resolution images or wrap their content in cumbersome download forms to track usage, creating friction that discourages reuse.

Digitisation is often legally blocked because scanning or copying an artwork, book, or photograph constitutes making a “copy” under copyright law. GLAM institutions often cannot digitise works unless they can find (or pay) the copyright holders. Because of these constraints, institutions sometimes digitise items based on the ease of rights clearance rather than cultural significance. Coad (2019) describes this phenomenon as the "digital skew," where our online cultural memory is dominated by older, public domain works, while modern history remains hidden behind copyright walls. This creates a distorted view of history where the "digital public domain" does not accurately reflect our recent past.

This distortion is not solely legal; it is also structural. In their quantitative analysis of Google Arts & Culture, Kizhner et al. (2021) introduce the concept of “digital cultural colonialism”. They demonstrate that despite the platform’s global reach, more than 90% of the its content originates from institutions in the USA and Western Europe. Specifically, the “LIFE Photo Collection” alone accounts for nearly three-quarters of the platform’s objects, heavily skewing the “global” memory towards a 20th-century American perspective. Furthermore, even within represented nations like Russia or France, they observed a “centre-periphery” bias, where capital cities dominate while provincial culture remains invisible. This suggests that aggregators do not merely reflect global heritage but actively reconstruct a Western-centric canon, reinforcing historical power dynamics in the digital realm.

Interactive Figure 2 shows a striking example of digitisation unlocking cross-cultural history is the collection of early Qing dynasty plant paintings held at the British Library. Recently analysed by Coulton (2025), these images were acquired in Xiamen (historically known as Amoy) around 1698–1702 by commercial travellers like James Cuninghame. Unlike later 'export art,' these 2,400 paintings represent a unique synthesis of Chinese bencao (materia medica) and huaniaohua (the flower-and-bird painting tradition), created by local artists specifically for European naturalists like Sir Hans Sloane. While they have languished in physical storage for centuries, IIIF technology now allows us to examine the delicate 'boneless' style brushwork and original Chinese nomenclature in minute detail, revealing a complex network of early modern scientific exchange that was previously difficult to access.

Digitisation also enables digital repatriation, which can be a powerful tool for indigenous communities seeking access to cultural artefacts that cannot be physically returned.

For instance, since 2024, the laboratory for the digitisation of oracle bones at Anyang Normal University in China has launched a major project. Its aim is to apply systematic methods to visit museums around the world, digitise their oracle bone collections, and integrate the resulting digital data into the project’s database, with the support and cooperation of those museums.

For three-dimensional objects, platforms like Sketchfab have become instrumental for the digital distribution of 3D models. Major GLAM institutions, including the British Library, use it to host and embed high-fidelity digital assets, allowing users to interactively examine sculptures, archaeological finds, and other artefacts directly in their web browsers. The object below is one such example: a revised edition (1621) of the 3D model of a Terrestrial Globe originally published in 1606 by Willem Janszoon Blaeu (1571–1638).

Such works cannot be realistic without the extensive digitisation efforts undertaken by museums and cultural institutions. However, we must remain critical of the process, ensuring that digitization efforts prioritize data quality, interoperability, and legal frameworks that allow for true accessibility, rather than creating new digital silos.
