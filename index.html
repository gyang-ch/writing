Thoughts on Topics in the DH6033 Course

An essay on TEI, Open Access, and GLAM Digitisation

This essay will discuss topics in the course DH6033. While there are many Digital Humanities related concepts in the course, I will focus on these three: TEI-XML and other text encoding scenarios, the problems and controversy of open access, and digitisation in the GLAM sector. They are either of interest to me, or have a quite debatable situation.

1. TEI and XML

Markup languages are the tools we use to structure text digitally. While HTML is all about how data looks on a screen, XML (eXtensible Markup Language) is designed to store and transport data. It focuses on the meaning and hierarchy of the text rather than its visual appearance.

XML is actually used everywhere in our digital lives, even if we don't see it. For example, standard files like .docx, .xlsx, and .pptx are really just ZIP folders full of XML files. If you rename a .docx file to .zip and extract it, you can actually see the collection of XML files that make up your document.

In Digital Humanities, the Text Encoding Initiative (TEI) is the main standard for using XML. However, there are several other frameworks depending on what you are trying to encode:

  • EpiDoc – A version of TEI XML made specifically for ancient documents like inscriptions and papyri.
  • MEI (Music Encoding Initiative) – A community project that defines a TEI-like system for encoding music notation.
  • IIIF (International Image Interoperability Framework) – This isn't a markup language itself, but it’s a standard that helps institutions share and present high-quality images and manuscripts smoothly.
  • YAML / JSON for born-digital archives – These are lighter formats that machines can read easily. Reimann et al. (2024) [cite_start][cite: 1664] argue that capturing born-digital data—like online forum threads full of emojis and upvotes—often needs custom schemas. These schemas need to handle the nested, tree-like structure of digital conversations, which traditional linear text encoding struggles with.
  • Text mining corpora – These often use "stand-off" annotation formats instead of embedding TEI directly, which allows researchers to add multiple layers of interpretation without breaking the XML structure.
TEI preserves scholarly nuance, but it is labour-intensive, which means large-scale TEI projects are more likely to be accomplished by well-resourced institutions.

It is also important to note that TEI isn't static; it changes to meet new technical and social challenges. Bauman et al. (2024) [cite_start][cite: 2161] point out that maintaining the tools that process TEI (the ODD processor) is technically difficult, leading to new projects like "ATOP" that aim to rebuild these tools from scratch. At the same time, the guidelines are evolving to be more inclusive. Beshero-Bondar et al. (2024) [cite_start][cite: 2460] describe recent updates that move away from binary definitions of sex and gender, allowing encoders to describe people in digital editions in a way that is more culturally accurate.

2. Open Access – The Internet’s Own Boy

The documentary The Internet’s Own Boy is a really strong introduction to the problems with how academic knowledge is produced. It shows how copyright laws often restrict access to journal articles. These rights usually belong to companies like Elsevier and JSTOR, not the people who actually did the work. This raises a big ethical question: is it fair for publishers to sell knowledge that was mostly paid for by public funding and academic labour?

The current system creates a divide. If you are part of a well-funded university, you can access library subscriptions easily. But the general public and independent researchers are effectively locked out. While moving towards Open Access tries to fix this, the transition is complicated financially.

Specifically, the "Gold Open Access" model, which often relies on Article Processing Charges, might actually create new unfairness. Papillon et al. (2019) [cite_start][cite: 4133] argue that while this model makes things readable for the public, it shifts the cost to the author. This could exclude researchers in the humanities and social sciences who don't have large grants, as well as junior scholars and those in the Global South. It risks becoming a "pay-to-publish" system where only well-funded voices are heard.

It is also important to distinguish between different types of scholarship. While journal articles don't usually make money for their authors, books are different. Monographs take years to write, and authors often rely on royalties as part of their income. Because of this, it is more ethically complicated to demand that books should be free compared to journal articles. However, academic books are so expensive that many students resort to "shadow libraries" like Z-Library, even though these sites exist in a legal grey area.

The definition of what counts as a "research output" also complicates things, especially in the GLAM sector. [cite_start]Rees Koerner and Ackrell (2025) [cite: 5033] note that while universities mostly produce text, museums produce things like exhibitions, gallery labels, and engagement activities. Traditional digital repositories often can't handle these kinds of outputs well, so we need new infrastructure to ensure museum research is as open and accessible as university research.

This creates a bit of a personal dilemma: as someone who consumes intellectual resources, I love the immediate access that digital platforms provide. But I am cautious about supporting systems that might hurt authors financially. These differences show that Open Access can't just be one single model; books and non-traditional outputs need their own sustainable approaches that balance access with the economic reality of producing them.

3. Digitisation in the GLAM sector

Digitisation in GLAMs provides many benefits. For example, it really helps with research. Over the past year, I have been very interested in two projects by visualisation researchers: OldVisOnline and ZuantuSet. They collect images of historical visualisations from digital collections all over the world.

Zhang et al. (2023) note that historical visualisations are often scattered across different libraries, museums, and archives, and they all have different metadata. This makes a unified dataset crucial for studying the history of science. Furthermore, Mei et al. (2025) highlight a big gap in digital humanities: the lack of non-Western materials. Their ZuantuSet focuses specifically on Chinese historical visualisations, challenging the Eurocentric bias we often see in general datasets.

[cite_start]However, even after thirty years of investment, only about 22% of heritage collections in Europe are actually digitised (Terras, 2022)[cite: 79]. Even when objects are digitised, they can be hard to find or use because of poor metadata. [cite_start]Lim and Liew (2011) [cite: 4576] found that while many institutions use basic standards like Dublin Core, the lack of technical metadata and the reliance on proprietary systems creates "silos." This means data can't be easily shared or harvested. If the metadata isn't compatible, these digitised items are basically invisible.

Digitisation itself uses a range of technical methods. Traditional techniques include 2D scanning for flat objects like manuscripts, high‑resolution photography for artworks, and multispectral imaging to reveal faded text. More advanced projects use photogrammetry, which builds 3D models from photographs, or laser scanning to create highly accurate 3D meshes of sculptures or artefacts.

While these technologies are impressive, they have limitations. Tausch et al. (2019) [cite_start][cite: 5430] describe how difficult it is to automate 3D digitization for large collections, especially for shiny objects like gold which need special techniques. Also, just because images exist doesn't mean they are easy to use. Valeonti et al. (2019) [cite_start][cite: 3367] note that many so-called "Open Access" museums still provide low-resolution images or hide them behind long download forms to track usage, which discourages people from actually using them.

Digitisation is often blocked by legal issues because scanning an artwork counts as making a "copy" under copyright law. GLAM institutions often can't digitise works unless they can find and pay the copyright holders. Because of this, institutions sometimes prioritize items that are easy to clear legally rather than what is culturally significant. [cite_start]Coad (2019) [cite: 584] calls this the "digital skew." It means our online cultural memory is full of older, public domain works, while modern history remains hidden behind copyright walls. [cite_start]This creates a distorted view of history that doesn't accurately reflect our recent past[cite: 582].

Digitisation also enables digital repatriation. This can be a powerful tool for indigenous communities to access cultural artefacts that cannot be physically returned to them.

However, we must remain critical of the process, ensuring that digitisation efforts prioritize data quality, interoperability, and legal frameworks that allow for true accessibility, rather than creating new digital silos.
