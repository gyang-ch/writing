Thoughts on Topics in the Digital Humanities Course

An essay on TEI, Open Access, and GLAM Digitisation

This essay will discuss topics in the course, reflecting on TEI-XML and other text encoding scenarios, the problems and controversy of open access, and digitisation in the GLAM sector.

1. TEI and XML

HTML and XML are both mark-up languages, and there are other mark-up languages such as YAML. Different from HTML, which is based on XML, XML is only targeted at storing text information, preserving the hierarchical traits of the text, but not containing anything about displaying the text.

XML is used in many places in today’s digital lives of people. For instance, files like .docx, .xlsx, .pptx are essentially ZIP archives containing many XML files. If you rename a .docx file to .zip and extract it, you will find a collection of XML files.

Beyond TEI-XML, there are other encoding frameworks:

  • EpiDoc – an XML standard built on TEI, used for inscriptions, papyri, and ancient documents.
  • MEI (Music Encoding Initiative) – a TEI-like encoding for music notation.
  • IIIF (International Image Interoperability Framework) – not a mark-up language, but a standard enabling rich digital presentation of manuscripts and images across institutions.
  • YAML / JSON for born-digital archives – used increasingly in digital asset management systems because they are lightweight and machine-friendly.
  • Text mining corpora – often encoded in stand-off annotation formats rather than TEI to allow multiple layers of annotation.
TEI preserves scholarly nuance, but it is labour- intensive, which means large-scale TEI projects are more likely to be accomplished by well-resourced institutions.

2. Open Access – The Internet’s Own Boy

I learnt a lot from the problem discussed in this film, which I think is about the accessibility of research journal articles, which is also related to copyright issues.

After watching the film and engaging in our class, my understanding now is that the copyright of journal articles usually belongs to the publishers (e.g. Elsevier, JSTOR). Am I right? Then the question is whether it is fair for the publishers to charge.

The current inequality in access to knowledge for the public and scholars in educational institutions is in part because publishers charge for access. Scholars in institutions have privileged access because their institutions subscribe. I agree that we should find a better model to give more equal access to knowledge to everyone. I also know that some scholars use funding to pay APCs so their publications can be open access.

Beyond journal articles, I am also concerned about the status of books in the open-access debate. While journal articles typically generate no income for their authors—who are usually salaried researchers—books belong to a different economic and labour structure. Monographs often represent years of work, and authors may rely, even modestly, on royalties as part of their income. This makes the ethics of providing free access to books more complicated than those surrounding journal articles. At the same time, students and independent learners frequently struggle with the high cost of academic books, which partly explains the appeal of websites like Z-Library, even though many of these platforms operate illegally. My own experience reflects this tension: as a consumer of intellectual resources, I appreciate the accessibility that piracy provides, yet I remain cautious about participating in it because of the legal risks and the potential impact on authors whose livelihood may depend on book sales. These differences suggest that open access cannot adopt a single, uniform model, and that books require a more nuanced and sustainable approach.

Table: Book vs Journal Publishing
Aspect Books (Monographs) Journal Articles
Typical economic model Sales and royalties; university press subsidies; occasional funder support for OA monographs Subscription revenue for publishers, or APC-funded open access
Author income May receive royalties (small to moderate); often important to some authors Authors typically receive no royalties
Typical funding Often unfunded; long-term research may need grants for travel/archives Often produced as part of funded projects or salaried roles
APCs / OA costs OA monograph fees can be very high (£5k–£12k+); models are less standardised APCs common for Gold OA (variable; often lower than OA monographs)
Production timescale Years of work, long editorial process Months to 1–2 years; faster turnaround
Rights & licensing Royalties and subsidiary rights matter; contracts more complex Authors usually transfer some or all rights; licensing often simpler
Equity implications High OA costs risk excluding unfunded scholars; sales-based models also make access unequal APCs can exclude unfunded researchers; Green OA helps but is uneven
Piracy impact Directly reduces sales/royalties; can harm authors on whom royalties matter Sci-Hub-style access undermines paywalls but doesn't provide a sustainable publishing model
Recommended OA approach Develop funding pools, national support for OA monographs, and experiment with library-publish models Mix of Green OA, transformative Read-and-Publish deals, and well-governed Gold OA

As a consumer of intellectual resources, such as books, journals, music, films, I would prefer them to be free, and I would enjoy using special technical skills to obtain them, but I wouldn't dare to circulate them in fear of legal risks.

3. Digitisation in the GLAM sector

The digitisation in GLAMs provides many benefits. For example, it helps with scholarly studies. In the past year, I have been obsessed with two research works by a group of visualisation (as a part of computer science) researchers. Their works OldVisOnline and ZuantuSet retrieve images about historical visualisations from major digital collections around the world and build them as a database. Their work, targeted at the history of visualisation, can be viewed in the discipline of history of science. Such works cannot be realistic without great digitisation efforts by museums and cultural institutions.

Digitisation is often legally blocked because scanning or copying an artwork, book, or photograph constitutes making a “copy” under copyright law. GLAM institutions often cannot digitise works unless they can find (or pay) the copyright holders. Because of these constraints, institutions sometimes digitise items based on the ease of rights clearance rather than cultural significance, producing a distorted, unrepresentative online cultural memory.

Digitisation also enables digital repatriation, which can be a powerful tool for indigenous communities seeking access to cultural artefacts that cannot be physically returned. Digital surrogates, 3D imaging, and shared databases allow dispersed cultural property to be reassembled virtually, supporting community recovery and research. For example, beginning last year, a group working on oracle bones at Anyang Normal University in China has visited museums worldwide to digitise oracle bones stored there, creating a distributed digital corpus for researchers and descendant communities.
